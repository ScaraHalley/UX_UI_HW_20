NAA Website Redesign

The National Archives of Australia


The Problem:

The National Archives of Australia (NAA) are difficult to navigate without a pre-existing understanding of their services THE

Solution:

Redesign the NAA User Interface and refine the information architecture to ensure the site can be navigated by a new user

My Role:

UI designer (Individual Project)

Tools:

Adobe XD, InVision, Miro

Research

The NAA collects Australian Government records, manages them, preserves them and makes them public. The purpose is so the public can access evidence of government decisions and actions, making them transparent and accountable.

Heuristic Evaluation

The NNA site could improve greatly, its purpose is unclear and the accessibility rating is below the current standard at only AA, and multiple methods are used to present information chunks to the user which adds to confusion when interacting with the site.

Accessibility Rating

The current site has a Accessibility Rating of AA, which does not meet current standards of accessibility. The goal of this project is to redesign the site to increase usability to the current standard (Accessibility Rating AAA) and boost interaction with services from the public

Stakeholder Research

I reached out to the NAA to understand their needs of the site, and what they want the public to get out of using their services. RecordSearch is complicated to use, NAA employees are concerned it will be hard for them to use if its made easier for the public to use. Archivists are often needed to provided 1-on-1 support to the public, which takes them away from their other work.

To avoid the scope of the project to expand beyond the deadline, focus for now will just be on the site’s overall navigation in mobile and desktop devices.

Proto Persona

The Proto Persona we designed is based of the older user, who has an interest in researching personal family history. They are less tech savvy than a younger user, and use their desktop computer to access the site when doing their research.

to this the focus of our research this round will be the desktop version of the site and making sure the design is responsive to different screen sizes. Although it would be valuable to look into the mobile version in later iterations

Information Architecture

The way information is organised at the top of the screen, is very different compared to the bottom of the screen. When comparing them the bottom navigation is much more logical, grouping categories by the different user types. Our hypothesis is organising the top navigation

Open Card Sorting

During the card sorting process there were common groupings, but as shown in our User Tests of the website navigation, the users were unsure what a lot of headings meant, especially the headings for the government. The conclusion drawn from testing was the we need to break down the information architecture into 3 main groups for Researchers, for Schools & Teachers and for Government users, and create tertiary navigation to group pages within the same categories

Sitemap Redesign

Based on card sorting results, Adobe XD was used to redesign the site information architecture to follow a similar flow to the bottom navigation.

Breaking the headings to the site resources into the three user categories: researcher, school & teachers and government.

Links were included to pages that had only been accessible from the bottom navigation in the top navigation.

Some of the secondary headings were moved to create tertiary headings to order the information in a way that is easier for users to understand at a glance

Navigation Interface Prototypes

During testing the users would always navigate from the top menu bar and browse their options. The longline dropdown of the menu has been used for a more comfortable browsing experience. With the indented tertiary menu to make categories clear without hiding information from users

Desktop Navigation Iteration

The header navigation was separated from the top of the screen to stand out to the user and styles and transitions were added

Maintaining tertiary headings as visible with secondary headings, aim is to minimise lostness with user

Will be incorporating this within the mobile prototypes as well and testing the effectiveness

Mobile Navigation Wireframes

Keeping in mind the age and tech savviness of our proto-persona 2 versions of the mobile wireframe were created to compare usability and access task flow in AB testing

Phone Navigation A B outcomes

Test A was the preferred 80% of the time when discussing ease of navigation, when Test B was preferred the user took much longer to understand Test B and quickly navigated through Test A

Homescreen layouts were tied on preference, service buttons should be stacked for accessibility

Having the sitemap located within the overlay menu was a failure and should stay located at bottom of the screen

Outcomes reinforced the usefulness of having the tertiary menu visible first as the user experienced more lostness when they couldn’t see the options first Will keep add a burger menu with a menu label to the Test A prototype and develop that design

Interaction Design

A modern color palette reminiscent of the Australian green and gold, that will pair nicely with the archival photographs was chosen. Which passes color usability testing with a AAA rating

Clear San Serif fonts chosen for the typography for easy reading, with a soft-white and soft-black to minimise eye strain of users.

Mobile & Desktop Usability Test

The Style guide was implemented on the Desktop Clickable and Type A mobile prototype to be tested by 8 users

Final Iterations

Key changes were made to the final interactions based of user feedback, and the prototypes were expanded to navigate away from the home page

  • menu font needed a heavier weight
  • Users couldn’t find Stories from the Archives in the top navigation
  • Burger and search menu icons positioning to be moved

What’s next ?

The goal of the current project were met, but based of the feedback from stakeholder research, RecordSearch is long overdue for an update, as it has been avoided as staff find it difficult to use and are worried updates may make some functions inaccessible

Current iterations should be tested with staff and research the functions needed by the government. The scope of the redesign can be expanded to the RecordSearch function to benefit both the needs of the NAA staff and the needs of the public
